🔍 How This Fence Calculator Works

Understanding Your Estimate

This calculator provides planning estimates based on standard industry practices and typical material requirements. Here's how we calculate your results:

Fence Length & Sections

▼

We divide your total linear footage by your selected post spacing to determine the number of fence sections. For example, 100 feet with 8-foot spacing creates approximately 12-13 sections.

Post Count Calculation

▼

We calculate line posts based on sections, then add corner posts, end posts, and gate posts based on your inputs. The formula is:

Line Posts = (Fence Length ÷ Post Spacing) - Corner Posts - End Posts - Gate Posts + 1

We typically recommend adding 1-2 extra posts for contingencies.

Concrete & Footings

▼

We estimate concrete volume based on your post hole depth and diameter. The formula calculates cubic feet per hole:

Volume = π × (radius²) × depth

A standard 10-inch diameter hole at 36 inches deep typically requires 2-3 bags of 50-lb concrete per post. We round up to ensure adequate coverage.

Picket Quantities

▼

We calculate pickets based on your fence length, picket width, and gap spacing:

Pickets = Fence Length × 12 ÷ (Picket Width + Gap)

For board-on-board or shadowbox styles, we multiply by the appropriate factor (1.5x to 2x). The waste percentage is then added to the total.

Waste Factor

▼

We apply a waste percentage (default 10%) to account for cuts, damaged pieces, and measurement variations. The calculator automatically adjusts this based on:

  • Terrain type (+2-5% for slopes)
  • Layout complexity (+3% for complex layouts)
  • Board orientation (+5-15% for diagonal/chevron patterns)
  • Number of gates (+2% for multiple gates)
  • Skill level (+3% for first-time DIY)

Cost Estimates

▼

Prices shown are based on average national pricing for typical materials. We use baseline costs that are then adjusted by:

  • Regional multipliers: Costs vary by region (0.90x to 1.25x)
  • Seasonal factors: Peak season may add 10-20% to labor
  • Material grade: Premium materials cost more
  • Pricing source: Lumber yards may offer 5-15% savings vs. retail

Actual costs vary significantly by location, supplier, season, and market conditions.

Assumptions Made

▼
  • Standard lumber dimensions and availability
  • Typical soil conditions suitable for standard post setting
  • Average installation complexity
  • Current market pricing (which fluctuates regularly)
  • No major obstacles (large rocks, utilities in path)

These calculations are meant to help you plan and budget. Always verify quantities and pricing with local suppliers before purchasing.

💡 Installation Tips

  • Call 811 at least 3 business days before digging
  • String line between corner posts for straight alignment
  • Set corner and end posts first - they're your reference points
  • Use a level on every post (plumb in two directions)
  • Let concrete cure 24-48 hours before attaching rails
  • Pre-stain or paint pickets before installation for complete coverage
  • Leave 2" gap at bottom to help prevent rot and allow drainage
  • Slope fence with terrain for racked style; step for stepped style
  • Start from a corner and work toward gates
  • Check local codes for height limits and setback requirements

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

How many fence posts do I need for 100 feet of fence? +

For 100 feet of fence with standard 8-foot spacing, you typically need 14 posts (100 ÷ 8 = 12.5 sections, plus 1 for the end = 13-14 posts). With 6-foot spacing, you typically need 18 posts. Add 2 extra posts for each gate. Always include corner and end posts in your count, and consider adding 1-2 extra for potential replacements. Actual requirements may vary based on your specific layout and site conditions.

How deep should fence posts be buried? +

Fence posts are commonly buried at least 2 feet deep, or approximately 1/3 of the total post length. In cold climates, it's typically recommended to dig below the frost line to help prevent heaving—often 36-48 inches in northern states. For a 6-foot privacy fence, 8-foot posts with 2 feet buried are commonly used. Post holes are typically 10-12 inches in diameter. Always verify local requirements and frost line depth for your area.

How many bags of concrete per fence post? +

For a standard 4×4 post in a 10-inch diameter hole that's 3 feet deep, typically 2-3 bags of 50-lb fast-setting concrete are commonly used. For 6×6 posts or deeper holes, 3-4 bags are often recommended. A 50-lb bag fills approximately 0.375 cubic feet. Fast-setting concrete typically allows you to continue work in 20-40 minutes. Actual requirements may vary based on soil conditions and hole dimensions.

How much does it cost to install 100 feet of wood fence? +

A 6-foot wood privacy fence typically costs between $1,500-$3,500 for 100 linear feet installed, though prices vary significantly by location and market conditions. Materials commonly run $800-$1,500 (posts, rails, pickets, concrete, hardware). Labor typically adds $700-$2,000 ($15-35 per linear foot). DIY may save 40-50% but requires proper tools. Vinyl fencing often costs 20-40% more; chain link typically costs 30-40% less. Always get local quotes for accurate pricing.

How many pickets do I need for a 6-foot privacy fence? +

For a 6-foot privacy fence using standard 5.5-inch wide dog-ear pickets with no gaps, you typically need approximately 2.2 pickets per linear foot (21-22 per 10-foot section). For 100 feet of fence, that's approximately 220 pickets plus 10% waste = about 242 pickets. Narrower 3.5-inch pickets typically require about 3.4 per foot. Actual quantities may vary based on picket width, spacing, and waste factor.

What is the best fence material for my area? +

The best fence material depends on your climate, budget, and maintenance preferences. For humid climates, vinyl or cedar are often recommended for rot resistance. For high-wind areas, consider metal or reinforced posts. For extreme cold, ensure posts are set below the frost line. Chain link is commonly used for security in various climates. Cedar and redwood naturally resist insects and decay. Consider consulting with local contractors or suppliers familiar with your specific regional conditions.

Do I need a permit to build a fence? +

Permit requirements vary significantly by location. Generally, permits are commonly required for fences over 6 feet tall, fences in front yards, pool enclosures, and properties in historic districts. Check with your local building department for specific requirements. Typical permit fees range from $50-500 depending on project size, but fees vary by jurisdiction. Working without required permits can result in fines and forced removal. Local building codes and HOA rules vary—always verify requirements before construction.

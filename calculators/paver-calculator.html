🪨 Base Preparation Guide

Proper base preparation is critical for long-lasting results:

Project Type Base Depth Total Excavation
Walkways/Patios 4" 7-8"
Pool Decks 4" 7-8"
Driveways (light) 6" 10-11"
Driveways (regular) 8" 12-13"
Commercial 8-12" 13-16"
Base Layers:
  • Compact subgrade to 95% density
  • Add gravel in 2" lifts, compact each
  • Final sand layer: 1" screeded, not compacted

👷 Pro Installation Tips

  • Slope for drainage: Minimum 1/8" per foot away from structures
  • Use string lines: For straight edges and consistent grade
  • Start from a corner: Begin at a 90° corner and work outward
  • Tap gently: Use rubber mallet to set pavers, don't pound
  • Check level frequently: Every 4-6 feet during installation
  • Wet saw for cuts: Clean, accurate cuts with less breakage
  • Dry pavers for sand: Apply polymeric sand only to completely dry pavers
  • Wait before sealing: 24-48 hours after polymeric sand
  • Keep extras: Store 5% extra pavers for future repairs
  • Don't rush base prep: This is the most important step

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping compaction: Each gravel layer must be compacted thoroughly
  • Insufficient base depth: Don't skimp on base, especially for driveways
  • No edge restraint: Pavers WILL shift without proper edging
  • Wrong sand type: Use concrete sand for bedding, not play sand
  • Wet polymeric application: Pavers must be completely dry
  • Ignoring drainage: Water must flow away from structures
  • Cutting corners on fabric: Landscape fabric prevents weed growth
  • Not checking for utilities: Call 811 before digging
📞
Always call 811 at least 48 hours before digging to locate underground utilities. It's free and required by law.

⏳ Polymeric Sand Guide

Why use polymeric sand?

  • Hardens when activated with water
  • Prevents weed growth between joints
  • Blocks insects from nesting
  • Resists erosion from rain
  • Locks pavers together for stability

Application Tips:

  • Pavers must be completely dry
  • Sweep sand into all joints thoroughly
  • Remove ALL excess from paver surface
  • Compact with plate compactor
  • Sweep again and fill any voids
  • Mist gently with water to activate
  • Keep dry for 24 hours after activation
⚠️
Excess polymeric sand left on paver surfaces will create a haze that's very difficult to remove.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about paver installation and costs

How many pavers do I need for a 10x10 patio? +
For a 10×10 foot patio (100 sq ft) using standard 4×8 inch brick pavers, you'll need approximately 450 pavers. This calculation includes the 4.5 pavers per square foot coverage rate plus a 10% waste factor. Using larger 12×12 inch pavers, you'd need about 110 pavers. Always add 5-10% extra for cuts, breakage, and future repairs. The exact number varies based on pattern choice—herringbone patterns require 10-15% more pavers due to additional cutting waste.
How much gravel do I need under pavers? +
For a typical patio, use 4 inches of compacted gravel base. For a 100 sq ft patio, you need approximately 1.25 cubic yards (about 1.75 tons) of crushed stone. Driveways require 6-8 inches of base, proportionally more material. Always add 1 inch of bedding sand on top of the compacted gravel layer. The base should extend 6 inches beyond the paver edge on all sides. For clay soils or areas with poor drainage, consider increasing the base depth by 2 inches.
What is the best pattern for a paver driveway? +
Herringbone at 45° or 90° is the best pattern for driveways. The interlocking V-shape provides superior resistance to the horizontal forces from vehicle traffic, tire torque, and turning. This pattern distributes weight across multiple pavers and prevents shifting. Running bond is suitable for light traffic areas, but herringbone is worth the extra 5-10% cutting waste for driveways. For commercial applications with heavy truck traffic, 90° herringbone with 3" thick pavers is recommended.
How much does a paver patio cost per square foot? +
Paver patios cost $10-$25 per square foot installed, depending on material and complexity. Materials alone (pavers, base, sand) run $4-$12 per square foot. Concrete pavers are most affordable ($3-8/sq ft), while natural stone costs $15-40/sq ft. Professional installation adds $5-15/sq ft. DIY can save 40-60% on labor costs. Factors that increase cost include complex patterns, curved edges, steps, multiple paver colors, and difficult site access.
Do I need edge restraint for pavers? +
Yes, edge restraint is absolutely essential. Without proper edge restraint, pavers will shift, spread, and separate over time, especially at the perimeter. Use plastic, aluminum, or steel paver edging secured with 10-inch metal spikes placed every 12 inches. For driveways, choose heavy-duty aluminum or steel edging to handle vehicle traffic loads. The only exception is when pavers butt against an existing concrete or stone structure that provides natural restraint.
How deep should I dig for a paver patio? +
Excavate to a depth that accommodates: gravel base (4" for patios, 6-8" for driveways) + sand bedding (1") + paver thickness (2-3") = 7-8" for patios, 10-12" for driveways. The finished paver surface should be level with or slightly above surrounding grade for proper drainage. Add 2" to excavation depth if you're installing landscape fabric or in clay soil conditions. Always slope the excavation 1/8" to 1/4" per foot away from structures.
What is polymeric sand and do I need it? +
Polymeric sand is specialized joint sand containing polymers that harden when activated with water, locking pavers together and preventing weed growth and insect intrusion. It costs more than regular sand ($20-30 vs $5-10 per 50lb bag) but provides superior performance. Highly recommended for all paver installations, especially in wet climates or areas prone to weeds. Coverage is typically 50-75 sq ft per bag depending on joint width. One caution: pavers must be completely dry during application.
Can I install pavers myself or do I need a professional? +
DIY paver installation is achievable for homeowners with moderate skills and proper preparation. Simple rectangular patios with running bond pattern are most DIY-friendly. You'll need to rent a plate compactor and paver saw (around $150-200 for a weekend). Professional installation is recommended for: driveways (critical base prep), complex patterns (herringbone, circular), slopes over 4%, areas larger than 500 sq ft, or if you're unsure about proper drainage. DIY saves $1,500-$5,000+ on a typical patio.
How long does paver installation take? +
A professional crew can install 150-200 sq ft per day including base preparation. DIY homeowners should expect 75-100 sq ft per day. A 300 sq ft patio takes 2-4 days DIY or 1-2 days professional. Add time for excavation (1 day for most projects) and any cutting-intensive patterns. Weather delays and learning curves should be factored in. The timeline breakdown: excavation (1 day), base prep (1 day), paver laying (1-2 days), cutting/finishing (0.5-1 day).
What size pavers should I use for a driveway? +
For driveways, use pavers at least 2.375" (60mm) thick for passenger vehicles and 3" (80mm) for heavier loads. Standard 4"×8" brick pavers in herringbone pattern are ideal as they interlock well under tire stress. Larger pavers (12"×12" or bigger) can crack under vehicle weight unless specifically rated for vehicular traffic. Avoid thin pavers (2" or less) for any driveway application. The thicker pavers distribute weight better and resist cracking under load.
How do I prevent weeds between pavers? +
Use multiple defenses: 1) Install landscape fabric under the gravel base, 2) Use polymeric sand in joints (hardens to block weed roots), 3) Ensure proper joint filling (no gaps), 4) Apply paver sealer to further protect joints, 5) Remove any weeds promptly before they seed. Polymeric sand alone prevents 95%+ of weed problems. For existing weed issues, use a non-selective herbicide, wait for weeds to die, remove, then re-sand joints with polymeric sand.
What's the difference between concrete and brick pavers? +
Concrete pavers are manufactured from cement and aggregates, available in many shapes, sizes, and colors, and cost $3-8/sq ft. Clay brick pavers are kiln-fired clay, limited to earth-tone colors, and cost $6-15/sq ft. Brick pavers last longer (100+ years vs 25-50), maintain color better (won't fade), but have fewer design options. Both perform well when properly installed. Concrete offers better value for most projects; brick is premium choice for traditional aesthetics.
Do I need a permit to install a paver patio? +
Requirements vary by location. Most jurisdictions don't require permits for ground-level patios under 200 sq ft. Larger patios, driveways, and any work affecting drainage may require permits. Factors that typically trigger permit requirements: projects over 200 sq ft, work within setback distances from property lines, drainage modifications, and work near easements. Always check with your local building department before starting. Permit fees typically range from $50-$300 for residential projects.
How do I calculate how much sand I need for pavers? +
For bedding sand: Square footage × depth (in feet) ÷ 27 = cubic yards. For a 300 sq ft patio with 1" sand bed: 300 × 0.083 ÷ 27 = 0.92 cubic yards (about 1.25 tons). For polymeric/joint sand: Divide square footage by coverage rate (50-75 sq ft per 50lb bag for polymeric, 80-100 for regular sand). A 300 sq ft patio needs approximately 4-6 bags of polymeric sand. Always round up and buy an extra bag for touch-ups.
What's the best time of year to install pavers? +
Fall is ideal for paver installation—cooler temperatures for physical work, stable weather, and end-of-season sales (15-25% savings). Spring is second-best but contractor schedules fill quickly. Summer works but is physically demanding and peak pricing applies. Winter installation is possible in mild climates but polymeric sand requires temperatures above 40°F. For DIY, avoid rainy periods as wet conditions make base preparation difficult. Professional contractors can work year-round with appropriate precautions.
Should I seal my pavers? +
Sealing is optional but beneficial. Benefits include: enhanced color (wet look or matte), easier cleaning, stain resistance, UV protection (prevents fading), and additional joint stabilization. Wait at least 30 days after installation before sealing. Reapply every 2-3 years. Cost is $0.50-$2.00 per sq ft DIY, $2-4 per sq ft professional. Best for: decorative patios, high-visibility areas, and light-colored pavers prone to staining. Skip sealing for: permeable pavers, very shaded areas, or budget-conscious projects.

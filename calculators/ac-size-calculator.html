🔍 How This AC Size Calculator Works

Understanding our calculation methodology and its limitations

Calculation Methodology

This calculator uses simplified load calculation formulas based on industry-standard methodologies to provide planning estimates. Here's how each factor is considered:

📐 Square Footage & Room Volume

We start with your room's square footage and multiply by a base BTU factor determined by your climate zone (ranging from 12-25 BTU per square foot). Ceiling heights above 8 feet increase the estimate proportionally because larger air volumes require more cooling capacity.

Assumption: Standard rectangular rooms with reasonably uniform ceiling heights.

🌡️ Climate Zone Impact

Climate zones significantly affect cooling requirements. We use cooling degree day (CDD) data to categorize locations into 7 zones. Hotter climates require more BTU per square foot due to greater temperature differentials.

Assumption: Based on typical design day conditions for each zone. Actual conditions vary year to year.

🏠 Insulation, Windows & Sun Exposure

Building envelope characteristics significantly affect heat gain. We apply adjustment factors for:

  • Insulation: Poor insulation increases load up to 30%; excellent reduces it up to 20%
  • Windows: Single-pane increases load; Low-E triple-pane reduces it
  • Sun exposure: South/west-facing rooms with full sun require up to 15% more cooling
  • Shading: Overhangs, awnings, and trees reduce solar heat gain

Assumption: Average window-to-wall ratios and typical construction methods for age category.

💧 Latent Load (Humidity)

Air conditioning removes both sensible heat (temperature) and latent heat (humidity). In humid climates, latent load can represent 30-40% of total cooling load. We estimate latent load based on climate humidity and moisture sources.

Assumption: Typical indoor moisture generation and ventilation rates.

👥 Internal Heat Gains

People, appliances, lighting, and electronics generate heat. We add BTU for occupants (250-700 BTU/person), kitchens (~4,000 BTU), electronics (500-5,000+ BTU), and lighting based on type.

Assumption: Typical occupancy during peak afternoon hours.

❄️ Understanding BTU and Tonnage

Essential cooling capacity terminology explained

What is BTU?

BTU (British Thermal Unit) measures the amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of one pound of water by one degree Fahrenheit. In air conditioning, BTU/hour indicates how much heat the unit can remove from a space per hour.

Higher BTU ratings mean more cooling power, but bigger isn't always better—proper sizing is essential for efficiency and comfort.

What is a Ton of Cooling?

One ton of cooling equals 12,000 BTU/hour. This measurement comes from the amount of heat needed to melt one ton (2,000 lbs) of ice in 24 hours.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about AC sizing, installation, and efficiency

For a 1,500 sq ft house in a moderate climate (Zone 3-4), a typical recommendation is 2.5 to 3 tons (30,000-36,000 BTU) for a central AC system. However, this varies significantly based on insulation quality, window types, ceiling height, sun exposure, and local climate.

Hot-humid climates may need 3-3.5 tons, while cooler regions may only need 2-2.5 tons. This calculator provides a more detailed estimate based on your specific conditions, but we always recommend professional evaluation for final sizing.

The general guideline is 20-25 BTU per square foot, but this varies considerably by climate zone:

  • Hot-humid areas (Florida, Texas Gulf Coast): ~25 BTU/sq ft
  • Hot-dry areas (Phoenix, Las Vegas): ~23 BTU/sq ft
  • Moderate climates (Atlanta, Dallas): ~22 BTU/sq ft
  • Cooler northern regions (Chicago, Boston): ~18 BTU/sq ft

These are starting points only. Add approximately 600 BTU per person above 2 occupants, 4,000 BTU for kitchens, and adjust for insulation quality, windows, and sun exposure.

An oversized AC causes "short-cycling" where it cools the space quickly, shuts off, then restarts frequently. This leads to several problems:

  • Energy waste: 10-20% more energy consumption from frequent starts
  • Poor humidity control: Not running long enough to remove moisture (cold but clammy)
  • Temperature swings: Uncomfortable fluctuations instead of steady temperatures
  • Increased wear: Compressor stress from frequent cycling shortens equipment life
  • Higher upfront cost: Oversized equipment costs more to purchase

It's actually better to be slightly undersized than significantly oversized. Variable-speed units handle slight oversizing better than fixed-speed units.

Generally yes, especially if you use AC frequently or live in a hot climate. A SEER 20 unit uses about 30% less energy than a SEER 14 unit.

Typical cost-benefit analysis (estimates only):

  • High-efficiency units cost $1,000-3,000 more upfront
  • Potential savings of $150-400 annually on cooling costs
  • Typical payback period: 4-8 years
  • Equipment life: 15-20 years (significant potential lifetime savings)

Additional benefits include better humidity control, more consistent temperatures, and quieter operation. Many also qualify for utility rebates and federal tax credits.

Window AC Units: Cost $150-700, DIY-friendly installation, SEER 10-15 typical, best for single rooms or temporary use. Blocks window and can be noisy (50+ dB).

Ductless Mini-Splits: Cost $1,500-5,000+ installed, professional installation required, SEER 16-30+ typical, very quiet (19-40 dB), better air filtration, heats and cools.

Choose window units for occasional/temporary cooling on a budget. Choose mini-splits for permanent, efficient cooling or when ductwork isn't feasible.

Consider a heat pump if: You need both cooling AND heating, live in moderate climate (temps rarely below 30°F), want to reduce fossil fuel use, or qualify for heat pump incentives.

Stick with AC-only if: You already have an efficient heating system, live in very cold climate (below 20°F frequently), or budget is the primary concern.

For cold climates, consider a "cold-climate heat pump" rated for low temperatures, or plan for backup heating.

Manual J is the HVAC industry standard methodology for calculating heating and cooling loads. It's a detailed room-by-room analysis that considers every factor: insulation R-values, window U-factors and SHGC, orientation, climate data, infiltration rates, internal gains, and duct losses.

While our calculator provides good estimates, a professional Manual J calculation is recommended for central AC systems, new construction, major renovations, or when energy efficiency is critical. Many quality HVAC contractors include this service.

Monthly costs depend on unit size, efficiency (SEER), hours of use, and electricity rates. Example for a 3-ton (36,000 BTU) central AC running 8 hours/day:

  • At SEER 14: ~$86/month (at $0.14/kWh)
  • At SEER 16: ~$76/month
  • At SEER 20: ~$60/month

Actual costs vary significantly by local electricity rates ($0.09-0.35/kWh), climate, thermostat settings, and home efficiency. These are estimates only.

Consider REPLACEMENT if: Unit is 10-15+ years old, repair costs exceed 50% of replacement value, uses R-22 refrigerant (phased out), energy bills rising, or frequent repairs needed.

Consider REPAIR if: Unit is under 10 years old, repair is minor (under $500), first major repair, no recurring problems, and unit still cools effectively.

The "$5,000 Rule": Multiply repair cost by age of system. If result exceeds $5,000, replacement is often more economical. Example: $600 repair × 10 years = $6,000 → Consider replacement.

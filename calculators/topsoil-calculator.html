How This Topsoil Calculator Works

This calculator provides planning estimates based on standard formulas and industry averages. Here's how we calculate your results:

Area Calculation

We use standard geometric formulas for each shape:

  • Rectangle: Length × Width
  • Circle: π × radius² (or π × (diameter/2)²)
  • Triangle: (Base × Height) ÷ 2
  • Oval/Ellipse: π × (Length/2) × (Width/2)
  • L-Shape: Sum of two rectangles
  • Custom: Direct square footage entry

Volume Calculation

We convert your area and depth into cubic yards using the industry-standard formula:

Cubic Yards = (Area in sq ft × Depth in inches) ÷ 324

Weight Estimates

We estimate weight based on typical soil density of approximately 1.0-1.3 tons per cubic yard for topsoil. Actual density varies by moisture content, composition, and compaction.

Buffer/Overage

We add a recommended buffer (typically 10-15%) to account for:

  • Settling and compaction over time
  • Irregular areas and measurement variations
  • Waste during spreading

Cost Estimates

Prices shown are based on national averages and regional data. We show ranges to reflect the significant variability in actual costs based on location, supplier, season, and soil quality.

Assumptions Made

  • Standard soil density (varies in reality)
  • Level application (slopes need adjustment)
  • Adequate site access (difficult access adds cost)
  • Current market pricing (which fluctuates)
  • No existing soil removal (add separately if needed)

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about calculating and ordering topsoil

The amount of topsoil you need depends on the area size and desired depth. Use this formula: Cubic Yards = (Length in ft × Width in ft × Depth in inches) ÷ 324.

For example, a 10×20 foot area with 4 inches of topsoil needs approximately 2.5 cubic yards. Our calculator makes this easy by handling all the math and including a recommended buffer for settling.

Most lawn projects need 4-6 inches of topsoil, while gardens typically need 8-12 inches for healthy root growth.

A standard pickup truck bed typically holds 1 to 1.5 cubic yards of topsoil, while a full-size truck with an 8-foot bed can hold about 2 cubic yards.

However, weight is often the limiting factor. One cubic yard of moist topsoil weighs approximately 2,000-2,600 pounds, which may exceed your truck's payload capacity. Always check your vehicle's payload rating before loading.

For larger quantities, bulk delivery is recommended and often more cost-effective.

Topsoil is the top layer of natural earth, screened to remove debris, and is best for filling, grading, and establishing lawns.

Garden soil is topsoil enriched with compost and amendments, ideal for flower beds and vegetable gardens.

Compost is fully decomposed organic matter used as a soil amendment to improve structure, drainage, and nutrients.

For most planting projects, a blend of topsoil and compost (often called garden mix or planting mix) provides the best results.

Topsoil prices typically range from $15-$50 per cubic yard for bulk delivery, depending on quality and location:

  • Unscreened topsoil: $15-$25/yard
  • Screened topsoil: $25-$40/yard
  • Premium garden blends: $35-$60/yard

Delivery fees usually add $50-$150 depending on distance. Bagged topsoil costs significantly more per volume but may be practical for small projects.

Prices vary by region, season, and supplier, so we recommend getting quotes from 2-3 local suppliers.

A cubic yard of topsoil typically weighs between 1,800 and 2,600 pounds (0.9 to 1.3 tons), depending on moisture content:

  • Dry, loose topsoil: 1,800-2,000 lbs/cu yd
  • Moist topsoil (typical): 2,200-2,400 lbs/cu yd
  • Wet/saturated topsoil: 2,400-2,800 lbs/cu yd

When planning delivery and calculating driveway weight loads, use approximately 1 ton per cubic yard as a planning estimate.

For establishing a new lawn, you typically need 4-6 inches of quality topsoil over prepared subsoil:

  • Seeding: 4 inches minimum, 6 inches preferred for better root development and drought resistance
  • Sod: 4-6 inches provides adequate root zone depth
  • Topdressing/repair: ¼ to ½ inch of fine topsoil or compost, ideally after aeration

The existing soil should be loosened and the topsoil blended at the edges to prevent layering.

The best times to add topsoil are:

  • Early spring (after frost but before active growth) - Excellent for lawns
  • Early fall (6-8 weeks before first frost) - Often ideal, especially for cool-season grasses

These periods offer moderate temperatures and typically adequate moisture for establishment. For gardens, early spring allows time for soil to settle before planting.

Avoid adding topsoil during extreme heat, when soil is frozen, or during heavy rain periods. The soil should be slightly moist but not wet when working with it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Mulch?

Mulch is a layer of material applied to the surface of soil around plants, trees, and garden beds. It provides numerous benefits for your landscape and is one of the most effective ways to maintain healthy plants while reducing maintenance.

✨ Key Benefits

  • • Retains soil moisture, reducing watering needs by 25-50%
  • • Suppresses weed growth naturally
  • • Regulates soil temperature in summer and winter
  • • Prevents soil erosion from wind and rain
  • • Improves soil fertility as organic mulch decomposes
  • • Creates a polished, professional landscape appearance

📏 Recommended Depths

  • • 2 inches: Annual refresh for existing beds
  • • 3 inches: New beds (recommended standard)
  • • 4 inches: New plantings, weed-prone areas
  • • 6 inches: Playgrounds, erosion control
  • • Keep 3-6" away from plant stems and tree trunks
  • • Never exceed 4" for most landscape applications

💰 Cost-Saving Tips

Buying Smart

  • • Buy off-season (fall) for 20-30% discounts
  • • Purchase bulk if you need 3+ cubic yards
  • • Check for volume discounts at 5+ cu yd
  • • Compare Home Depot, Lowe's, and local nurseries
  • • Look for end-of-season clearance sales

Reduce Quantity Needed

  • • Use 2" depth for refresh vs 3" for new beds
  • • Account for existing mulch depth
  • • Use landscape fabric as base layer
  • • Edge beds properly to prevent migration
  • • Contact local arborists for free wood chips

⚠️ Common Mulching Mistakes

🌋 Volcano Mulching

Piling mulch against tree trunks causes rot, pest problems, and tree death. Keep 3-6" gap.

📏 Too Deep

Over 4" prevents water and air from reaching soil. Stick to 2-4" for most applications.

💧 Too Shallow

Under 2" won't suppress weeds or retain moisture effectively. Minimum 2" recommended.

🛒 Buying Too Much

Measure carefully and use our calculator. Extra mulch wastes money and storage space.

🌿 Wrong Type

Some mulches harm certain plants. Research compatibility before purchasing.

🥕 Dyed Near Edibles

Avoid dyed or rubber mulch near vegetable gardens. Use natural organic options instead.
